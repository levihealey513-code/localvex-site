Why Every Roofer in Cincinnati Should Be Running Google Ads Right Now

Roofing is one of the highest-intent search categories on Google. When someone types "roof repair Cincinnati" into their phone, they're not browsing. They're not doing research for a school project. They're standing in their kitchen staring at a water stain on the ceiling, and they need someone to fix it this week. That's a buyer. That's a person with a credit card and a problem, and they're going to hire whoever shows up first with a credible offer.

If you're a roofing contractor in the greater Cincinnati area — Hamilton County, Butler County, Clermont County, Northern Kentucky — and you're not running Google Ads, you are handing those calls to your competitors. Every single day. Not because they're better roofers than you. Because they showed up when the homeowner was searching, and you didn't.

Let me break down exactly how this works, what it costs, and what you can realistically expect to get from it.

How Google Local Service Ads Work for Contractors

Before we even get to traditional Google Ads, let's talk about Google Local Service Ads, or LSAs. If you've Googled anything like "plumber near me" or "roofer Cincinnati" recently, you've seen them. They're the ads that show up at the very top of the page — above even the regular paid ads — with a green "Google Guaranteed" checkmark.

LSAs are pay-per-lead, not pay-per-click. That means you only pay when someone actually contacts you through the ad — calls you, messages you, or books a service. For roofing, the cost per lead through LSAs in the Cincinnati market typically ranges from $30 to $80, depending on the time of year and competition level. During storm season? It's higher. During a slow January? It's lower.

The key advantage of LSAs for roofers is trust. That Google Guaranteed badge signals to the homeowner that you've been vetted — background checked, insured, licensed. In an industry where homeowners are genuinely nervous about getting scammed (and rightfully so — the roofing industry has a reputation problem), that badge is worth its weight in gold.

To get set up, you'll need to pass Google's screening process, which includes proof of insurance, a license check, and background checks for the business owner. It takes a couple weeks but it's straightforward. Once you're in, you set a weekly budget, and Google shows your ad to people searching for roofing services in your service area. Simple. Effective. And one of the highest-ROI marketing channels available to any roofing contractor right now.

What a Properly Structured Roofing Campaign Looks Like

Beyond LSAs, standard Google Search Ads are the next piece. This is where you bid on specific keywords so your ad shows up when someone searches for roofing-related terms. The structure matters. A lot. Here's what a well-built roofing campaign in the Cincinnati market looks like.

Campaign 1: Emergency / Repair. These are your highest-intent keywords. "Emergency roof repair Cincinnati." "Roof leak repair near me." "Storm damage roof Cincinnati." People searching these terms have an active problem and need help now. Your ad should reflect urgency: "24-Hour Roof Repair in Cincinnati — Call Now for Same-Day Service." The landing page should be a dedicated repair page with a click-to-call button and a simple form. Not your homepage. A specific page built for this exact search.

Campaign 2: Replacement / New Roof. Slightly lower urgency but much higher ticket value. Keywords like "roof replacement Cincinnati," "new roof cost Ohio," "best roofing company Cincinnati." These searchers are likely comparing options. Your ad needs to convey credibility and value: "Trusted Cincinnati Roofers — Free Estimates, Financing Available." The landing page should showcase reviews, before-and-after photos, and a clear call to action.

Campaign 3: Branded. Someone searching your exact company name. This is cheap — pennies per click — and protects you from competitors bidding on your brand name (which they absolutely will if you're any good). Always run a branded campaign.

For match types, we recommend a mix of phrase match and exact match for your core terms, with broad match used sparingly and only with smart bidding strategies that can filter out irrelevant traffic. Negative keywords are essential — you need to exclude searches like "roofing jobs," "roofing salary," "DIY roof repair," and "roofing materials wholesale." Without negatives, you'll burn 20 to 30 percent of your budget on clicks from people who will never hire you.

The Biggest Mistakes Cincinnati Roofers Make with Google Ads

Mistake #1: Sending all traffic to the homepage. Your homepage is not a landing page. It's a general introduction to your company. When someone searches "roof leak repair Cincinnati," they want to land on a page that talks specifically about roof leak repair, has a phone number and form above the fold, and makes it obvious what to do next. Sending them to a homepage with a slider, an "About Us" paragraph, and a list of 15 services is the fastest way to waste a $25 click.

Mistake #2: No call tracking. If you're running Google Ads and you don't have call tracking set up, you have no idea which ads, keywords, or campaigns are generating actual phone calls. You're flying blind. Proper call tracking assigns a unique phone number to your Google Ads traffic, records the calls, and attributes them back to the specific keyword that triggered the ad. This data is how you optimize. Without it, you're guessing.

Mistake #3: Setting it and forgetting it. Google Ads is not a "set it and forget it" platform. Keyword performance shifts. Competitors enter and exit the auction. Seasonal demand changes everything. A campaign that's profitable in April might hemorrhage money in December if nobody's adjusting bids, pausing underperformers, and testing new ad copy. You need someone managing it weekly — either you, or an agency that actually knows roofing.

Mistake #4: Bidding on too many broad keywords. "Roofing" as a standalone keyword will attract every type of search imaginable — people looking for roofing materials, roofing jobs, roofing DIY videos, roofing tiles for their Minecraft house. You'll pay $15 a click and convert zero of them. Specificity wins. "Roof replacement Mason OH" is worth ten times more than "roofing."

What $1,500/Month in Ad Spend Can Realistically Generate

Let's run the real math for a roofing company in the Cincinnati market.

Monthly ad budget: $1,500. This is a realistic starting point for a small to mid-size roofing contractor. It's enough to generate meaningful data and real leads without breaking the bank.

Average cost per click (CPC): $15 to $35 for competitive roofing keywords in Cincinnati. Let's use $25 as a middle estimate.

Total clicks per month: $1,500 / $25 = 60 clicks.

Landing page conversion rate: A well-built landing page converts at 8 to 15% for roofing. Let's use 10% — conservative but realistic if the page is properly optimized.

Leads per month: 60 clicks × 10% conversion = 6 leads.

Close rate: Most roofing companies close 25 to 40% of qualified leads. Let's use 30%.

Jobs booked: 6 leads × 30% = roughly 2 new jobs per month.

Average job value: In the Cincinnati market, a roof replacement averages $8,000 to $15,000. Let's use $10,000.

Monthly revenue from Google Ads: 2 jobs × $10,000 = $20,000.

Return on ad spend: $20,000 revenue / $1,500 ad spend = 13.3× return.

Now, these are estimates. Your numbers will vary based on your market, your sales process, your pricing, and the quality of your landing page and ad copy. But the fundamental math holds: roofing is a high-ticket service with high search intent, which means even a modest ad budget can generate a massive return if the campaign is structured correctly.

And here's the kicker — those numbers improve over time. As you collect conversion data, Google's algorithm gets smarter about who to show your ads to. Your quality score improves. Your cost per click drops. Your conversion rate increases. Month three is always better than month one.

If you're a roofer in Cincinnati, Butler County, Clermont County, or Northern Kentucky, Google Ads is not optional anymore. It's where your customers are looking. The only question is whether your ad is the one they see, or your competitor's.
